# Bringing Things down to Earth

### A Rotovator as Rocket Lab's First Tether Business - can it pay for itself?
Essay 2 of 4 on harvesting orbital momentum
___

I ended my last essay with a question. I argued that orbital momentum is an unpriced asset, that throwing it away as heat at the end of a satellite's life is a design flaw dressed up as responsible behavior, and that a company with the right combination of spacecraft operations, tether heritage, and launch capacity might find a durable business standing in that waste stream. One should note that the salvage value in situ, of a satellite is primarlily its momentum - to recover the space aged components, or recovering the 30% of satellite mass that is typically aluminum would require enormous maturity of a space based manufacturing base to exploit. And the momentum salvage value is substantial.

Several readers responded, (thank you!) reasonably, and asked given that satellite momentum has real value, what the physical reality of a capture system might actually looks like. This is my attempt to go one layer deeper — not to prove the case, yet, but to show the geometry of it more clearly. How many rotovators would be required?

And to address my most pressing question - can one make a business case for avoiding the “tragedy of the commons” for our upper atmosphere and Low Earth Orbit?


## What a rotovator actually does
A momentum-exchange tether in its simplest form is just two masses connected by a long cable, orbiting together and rotating slowly around their common center of mass. That is already doing something interesting: the tip of the rotating cable, at the right moment in its rotation, is moving faster or slower than orbital velocity for that altitude — which means it can meet an incoming spacecraft at a different speed than a free-flying object at the same altitude could.
A rotovator takes that logic and makes it the product. The facility rotates continuously. At the lower tip of its rotation, the arm moves slower than local orbital velocity — slow enough to match the relative speed of an ascending payload and catch it, then carry it upward through the rotation arc until, at the upper tip, it releases the payload moving faster than local orbital velocity and therefore boosted to a higher orbit. Nothing is burned. The rotation does the work that propellant would otherwise do.
Run the machine in the other direction and the same geometry serves deorbit: the upper tip catches an outgoing satellite moving with full orbital velocity, absorbs some of that momentum into the facility's spin, and releases it at the lower tip moving slower than reentry velocity — slow enough, potentially, for an inflatable heat shield to handle the remainder without burning the satellite to aluminum dust. The catch is the hard part. The tip must arrive within meters and within milliseconds of the target satellite — a precision rendezvous problem of exactly the kind that Northrop Grumman's MEV already solves commercially in GEO, and precisely what a spacecraft rendezvous and proximity operations course teaches in careful mathematical detail. The physics is understood. The engineering is demanding but not exotic.
What the geometry cannot capture is what the tether feels in the moment of capture: a sudden load transmitted through hundreds or thousands of meters of cable as a traveling mechanical wave. The structural challenge is not strength alone but elasticity and damping — the tether must absorb the jolt without snapping, the way a cable-stayed bridge absorbs the load of a heavy truck crossing at speed. A viscoelastic tether, designed to behave more like a bungee cord than a steel cable, dissipates the oscillation energy as heat in the material rather than as destructive vibration. That is, again, a solved engineering problem in terrestrial structures. Translating it to orbit, in vacuum, under radiation, over thousands of operational cycles, is where the real work is.
## How many?
One rotovator in orbit would not provide much value. The primary way of thinking about rendezvous in orbit is first you match the orbital plane. This is extraordinally expensive in LEO (Low Earth Orbit), A simple rule of thumb is that changing your orbital plane by 60 degrees will require the same amount of delta-v as launching from earth to that orbital plane. This is one reason why Rocket Lab can sell launch on its smaller Electron rocket when SpaceX is offering cheap ride shares. Getting to a sub optimal orbit and moving is time consuming and expensive. One might change the orbital plane of a rotovator by capturing mass slightly out of your orbital plane. But one would have to balance that with the added complexity and risk to an already complex engineering problem. It might be more reasonable to think of that as only collecting and saving on some reaction mass.
How many rotovators would you need to provide a service? A useful operating assumption is that one vehicle should avoid more than about 2° to 5° of routine plane change between campaigns. At 5°, the chemical penalty is already about 25% of dry mass, while Hall propulsion keeps the propellant burden near 4% of dry mass, though at the cost of much slower transfers.
That makes the likely commercial pattern clear: one tug handles many targets in one shell, then occasionally migrates to another shell using electric propulsion, while the overall service quality comes from fleet distribution rather than from heroic maneuvering by a single craft.
* 3 to 4 vehicles in sun-synchronous orbit.
* 2 to 3 vehicles in mid-inclination commercial shells.
* 1 to 2 transfer or reserve vehicles using electric propulsion for slow redeployment.

⠀So the business stops being about heroic acrobatic salvage and becomes a problem of territorial coverage. A viable debris service would need a small flotilla of rotovators, each assigned to a crowded orbital province, because in LEO it is usually cheaper to duplicate presence *rather than to buy sideways motion with fuel*.
How much would you need to charge to have this make sense (in a back-of-the-envelope sense)?
My envelope model suggests that a general orbital janitorial service becomes interesting only if customers pay several million dollars per object and the janitoral vehicles are reused many times; below that, the economics struggle against high development and operations costs.
Market anchors
The clearest real-world anchor is ESA’s ClearSpace-1 contract, which was €86 million for removing a single 112 kg Vespa object. A separate market analysis citing Kall Morris says quoted removal prices ranged from about $4.0 million to $62.5 million per object for 52 large U.S.-owned debris items in LEO, which is a much broader and more commercially suggestive band. Those numbers show that probably early debris removal is not going to be a commodity service. It looks more like a high-value sovereign or *insurer-backed mission business*.
# The deorbit landscape: what else is on offer
So what are the other options for constellaton operators, other than the tragedy of the commons? The deorbit market is not waiting. There are several viable approaches, each with genuine commercial deployments today.
Onboard propellant burns remain the default. The satellite fires its thrusters retrograde at end of life and drops into the atmosphere. It works reliably, requires no external service, and keeps the operator in control. The cost is paid at launch: every kilogram of deorbit propellant is a kilogram that did not earn revenue during the satellite's operational life. It does not address the concerns about the health of our upper atmosphere.
Drag sails deploy a large membrane after the payload's useful life ends, increasing atmospheric drag and gradually lowering the orbit. They are passive, lightweight, and relatively inexpensive. Their limitation is time: a drag sail can take years to deorbit a satellite from typical LEO altitudes, and it gives the operator no control over the process once deployed.
Electrodynamic tethers are the closest cousin to rotovator logic. A conductive tape or wire is extended from the satellite; its interaction with Earth's magnetic field and the ionospheric plasma generates a braking force with no propellant and no moving parts. Tethers Unlimited's Terminator Tape, which Rocket Lab has already flown on an Electron rideshare, is the leading commercial example. It is elegant physics: the satellite's own orbital velocity drives a current through the tether, and that current creates a drag force that slows the satellite. The limitation is directionality — an electrodynamic tether bleeds momentum into the ionosphere as waste electrical current. It cannot transfer that momentum to another spacecraft.
Active space tugs — vehicles that dock with a dead satellite and fire thrusters to deorbit it — are the highest-capability option and the most expensive. Astroscale's commercial debris removal service is the leading example. The tug must match the target's orbit precisely, dock with a non-cooperative object, and carry its own propellant for the burn. The service fee reflects all of that complexity.
The rotovator occupies a position none of these fill: an external service that deorbits a satellite without requiring it to carry deorbit propellant, without destroying the satellite in the process, and without wasting the momentum it is removing. Every other approach either burns propellant or loses momentum as heat or current. The rotovator banks momentum. That distinction only matters commercially if there are enough satellites to deorbit to justify building the banking infrastructure — which brings us to the market.


# The market: how many satellites are we actually talking about
This is where the numbers stop being abstract. Starlink currently operates over 10,000 satellites in low Earth orbit, with FCC authorization for 15,000 and filings for up to 42,000. SpaceX has stated publicly that it plans to refresh the constellation roughly every five years with newer technology — meaning the first generation of Starlink satellites is already beginning its deorbit cycle. At a five-year refresh cadence, SpaceX alone will be deorbiting on the order of 2,000 satellites per year at the current constellation size, rising steeply as the constellation grows. Independent analysts have estimated that at full planned scale, Starlink's natural attrition will produce approximately two tonnes of reentering satellite mass per day.
Amazon's constellation, now operating under the name Amazon Leo, is authorized for 3,236 satellites, with 700 expected in orbit by mid-2026. Eutelsat's OneWeb has 618 first-generation satellites in orbit at approximately 1,200 kilometers — notably higher than Starlink, meaning they will not passively deorbit through atmospheric drag in any reasonable timeframe and must actively maneuver to comply with end-of-life regulations. Eutelsat has just contracted Airbus to build a further 340 OneWeb satellites and is investing roughly 2 billion euros in its next generation. Blue Origin's TeraWave constellation, announced at the start of this year, plans 5,408 optically interconnected satellites in LEO and MEO. The European Union's sovereign IRIS² constellation is under development. China's Qianfan and GuoWang programs each contemplate thousands of satellites.
The combined LEO constellation population currently stands at roughly 10,000 active satellites and is on a trajectory toward 50,000 or more within the next decade. Every one of those satellites has an end of life. Every one of them currently has no better option than burning up or carrying its own deorbit fuel. The deorbit obligation is not a niche compliance problem — it is a structural feature of the largest infrastructure buildout in the history of spaceflight, and it has no elegant solution yet. And Elon is talking about millions of satellites…


# How the rotovator might earn its keep
The commercial logic of the rotovator follows directly from that market picture. Constellation operators face three deorbit cost structures simultaneously: the propellant mass they must reserve at launch, the operational life they sacrifice by retaining deorbit capability, and the regulatory and reputational exposure of non-compliance. An external deorbit service that eliminates the first two and guarantees the third is not selling a luxury — it is selling cost relief against an obligation that grows with the size of the constellation.
Pricing is not difficult to sketch at the level of general principles. If a constellation satellite costs roughly one million dollars to build, launch, and operate, and carries perhaps fifty kilograms of deorbit propellant that could otherwise have been revenue-generating payload mass, then eliminating that propellant mass across a constellation of ten thousand satellites is worth something in the range of hundreds of millions of dollars in recovered launch capacity — before accounting for operational life extension. But rotovator service that charges millions for a deorbit- on the same range as putting the constellation in orbit seems like a hard sell. But just as StarLink accounts for most of SpaceX launches, a tether system that is built into a vertically integrated Rocket Lab Flatellite system might have differernt economics.
Cooperative satellites can be designed from the start with grapple fixtures, known mass properties, standardized interfaces, passivation procedures, health telemetry, and even end-of-life capture modes. That sharply reduces rendezvous uncertainty, mission planning cost, legal complexity, and failure risk compared with chasing unknown derelicts that may be tumbling, fragmented, or poorly documented. And if Rocket Lab is designing the satellites that picture changes.
This matters because current debris-removal prices are high largely due to bespoke operations. If the target is your own hardware, in your own shell, built to be captured, the service starts to look more like spacecraft fleet maintenance than heroic salvage.
A company like Rocket Lab, if it operated a large cooperative constellation, would not need its rotovators to make money the way a standalone cleanup firm must; it would only need them to lower the total cost and risk of owning the constellation, just as SpaceX did not need Falcon 9 to be justified one launch at a time once it became the railroad for Starlink. But the argument becomes, does it make more sense to build some (as of now, undeveloped) on-board recovery system into each satellite rather than what is essentially building a full railroad, just for maintenace? Yes, but…
The deeper commercial argument, though, is not about *unit* economics. It is about what the facility becomes as the market grows. Every deorbit it performs adds angular momentum to the facility's rotation. That stored momentum is inventory. When the boost direction is added — when a new satellite needs a kick to a higher orbit — the facility sells the momentum it bought from the last deorbit customer to the next boost customer. The facility is not consuming propellant to provide either service. It is a clearinghouse for orbital velocity, and its margins improve *as its transaction volume grows*.
# What can change the business equation
If a rotovator could reliably move payloads from LEO toward GEO and charge for that service, the business case would improve substantially, because you are no longer relying only on debris removal, compliance, or reduced insurance premium. You are salvaging momentum and you gain a direct transportation revenue stream that customers already understand and pay for.
Hall thrusters can provide a very slow economical way to raise a LEO satellite to GEO. That time has a monetary value to commerial satellite operators. And defense use case often cannot tolerate this lag. It seems to me that a rotovator built to deorbit a cooperative failed Flatellite could provide a paying service of boosting LEO to higher orbits. This makes the business case much stronger. Can I predict that the business case will be compelling? No. Can I suggest that experiments along these lines, with existing electron launches might be justified? Yes.


# Why the rotovator builds MXER learning
The risk calculus of a rotovator investment, in my mind, needs to be balanced against the goal of a full MXER system. A full MXER system — one that both deorbits and boosts, restores its own angular momentum, and operates as a genuine orbital momentum bank — requires the development of several demanding subsystems: rendezvous and proximity operations for cooperative and non-cooperative targets, tether structural dynamics and libration control, tip-speed matching guidance systems, catch-and-release mechanisms, and electrodynamic reboost to restore the facility's energy between operations.
Every one of those subsystems must be developed anyway to build a commercially viable rotovator. The rotovator is not a simplified version of MXER — it is a small MXER with the boost direction switched off until the market justifies turning it on. The company does not need to commit to a full momentum-exchange bank. It needs to commit to brining things back to earth that would otherwise ruin our atmosphere, and let the engineering heritage accumulate naturally. Only then can we really say if a full size MXER system is justified.
Rocket Lab already has more of that heritage than is commonly acknowledged. They have operated the Photon spacecraft bus on missions requiring precise orbital insertion and long-duration operations. They have flown Tethers Unlimited's Terminator Tape, establishing a direct relationship with the leading tether hardware developer. Their Neutron vehicle, currently in development, will provide the mass-to-orbit needed to place a rotovator facility of commercially meaningful size. The gap between where Rocket Lab is and where a first rotovator demonstration needs to be is smaller than the gap between where SpaceX was in 2010 and where the Falcon 9 landed on a barge in 2015.
SpaceX did not announce a fully reusable rocket. It announced it was going to try to recover a booster, and it learned from the splashes. The ambition was always full reusability; the public commitment was to the next experiment. Rocket Lab could position a rotovator the same way: a commercial orbital deorbit service that happens to be building, one mission at a time, the infrastructure for something much larger.
