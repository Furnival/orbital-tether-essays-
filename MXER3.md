# From Nice-to-Have to Infrastructure

### Path to an orbital utility

My first essay on momentum tethers asked whether orbital momentum could be treated as inventory rather than waste. The second argued that a rotovator deorbit service is a viable first step — funded by the growing obligation of constellation operators to dispose of their satellites cleanly and by being a desireable feature on Flatellite systems for Rocket Lab itself and for its customers. This step giving Rocket Lab the capablity of building, one mission at a time, every subsystem that a full momentum-exchange system would need. This essay assumes that argument holds, and asks what comes next.
Below I speculate that a dozen Rocket Lab tether systems servicing deorbit missions, can become profitable when it can sell boosts to GEO as a service. When the facility has accumulated more angular momentum than its deorbit customers require, it could use that momentum to boost payloads to a higher orbit. This is not a question of physics. It is a question of whether there is a market willing to pay for the angular momentum the facility would have in inventory.
I believe it will. I want to try to show why.

# What the boost offers

To understand the commercial case, it helps to understand what a GEO satellite operator currently pays for, and what they are actually buying.
A communications satellite destined for geostationary orbit is typically launched to a geostationary transfer orbit — an elliptical path whose high point touches GEO but whose low point sits in LEO. Getting from that transfer orbit to the final circular GEO station requires the satellite itself to fire an apogee kick motor, consuming propellant that represents roughly forty to fifty percent of the satellite's total launch mass. That propellant did not generate revenue. It did not carry transponders or antenna aperture. It was dead weight that the rocket had to lift, and that the satellite had to carry, for the sole purpose of completing a job the launch vehicle left unfinished.
A tether boost facility changes that calculation. Instead of launching the satellite directly to GTO and having it finish the orbit-raising itself, the operator launches the satellite to a lower, cheaper orbit and pays the tether facility to toss it the rest of the way. The satellite arrives at GEO having consumed little or none of its apogee propellant — which means either the satellite can be made lighter and launched on a smaller vehicle, or the propellant mass can be replaced with payload capacity, extending the satellite's operational life by years. A satellite that flies lighter costs less to launch. A satellite that carries more transponders earns more revenue per year in orbit. The arithmetic runs in the same direction both ways.
The structural logic was worked out in detail in a NASA-funded study by Tethers Unlimited and Boeing, completed in 2001 and still the most rigorous publicly available design for this type of system. Their conclusion was that a tether boost facility could reduce the cost of delivering a payload to GEO *by fifty to eighty percent* on a per-mission basis, with potential for greater reductions as the system matures and traffic grows. The numbers are from a different era of launch pricing, but the structural argument has not changed: a reusable orbital facility that requires no propellant to operate will always undercut a rocket-based upper stage over sufficient transaction volume. Business, not physics.

## What the facility looks like

The Tethers Unlimited and Boeing design gives us a concrete reference point, which is useful because it grounds the argument in something more than analogy. Their modular first-generation facility was sized to boost 2,500 kilogram payloads from low Earth orbit to geostationary transfer orbit, with a throughput of approximately one payload per month. The entire facility was designed to launch on a single heavy-lift rocket — in 2001, a Delta IV-H — at a launch mass of just under 20,000 kilograms. It uses a 100-kilometer tether, roughly 80 kilometers of which is electrically conductive, powered by solar arrays producing approximately 100 kilowatts. After each boost operation, the facility restores its own orbital energy electrodynamically, using Earth's magnetic field and solar power. No propellant. No resupply missions. The energy inventory replenishes itself.
The reason I mention Neutron in the context of this design is not promotional. It is practical. A facility massing under 20,000 kilograms fits in the payload class that Neutron is being designed to reach. That means Rocket Lab could, in principle, manufacture and launch the first operational tether boost facility on its own vehicle, without dependence on another company's manifest. The vertical integration argument — build it, launch it, operate it — is not common in the aerospace industry, and it is worth noting when it appears.
The same facility, incidentally, can boost 1,000 kilogram payloads to lunar transfer orbit. That is not a footnote. For a company that has already expressed serious interest in deep-space missions, including Venus, which I will discuss in the next essay. The fact that the first commercial revenue-generating component of a tether transport architecture also doubles as a lunar transfer service is the kind of optionality that deserves a line in a strategic plan.

## The GEO market, smaller but valuable.

I want to be careful here, because the GEO market is smaller and slower than the constellation market, and it would be easy to overstate the revenue case.
The global market for new GEO communications satellites runs to roughly fifteen to twenty-five launches per year in recent years, with some variation depending on technology cycles and the competitive pressure from LEO broadband constellations. These are not high-volume transactions. They are *high-value ones*: a typical GEO communications satellite represents two hundred million dollars or more in total program cost, and the portion attributable to orbit-raising propellant and the launch vehicle capacity to carry it is a meaningful fraction of that figure.
The LEO constellation market is the larger number in volume but the more complex one commercially, because the operators — SpaceX, Amazon, Eutelsat — are also vertically integrated launch providers or have preferred launch arrangements. Selling a deorbit service to SpaceX requires Rocket Lab to compete against SpaceX's own operational preferences.
Selling a boost service to GEO satellite operators — who are mostly not launch providers and mostly do not have preferred tether arrangements — is a *cleaner commercial conversation*. The GEO satellite market already exists. The pain is already priced in — every operator knows exactly what fraction of their launch mass is propellant they'd rather not be carrying. What the market has lacked is a company willing to treat the deorbit business as the training wheels for the infrastructure, and use that experience to make a boost service possible and profitable.

## The business case in plain terms

I am not an investment banker and I am not going to pretend I can model this with precision. But the structural arithmetic is simple enough to sketch.
If the facility can process one GEO boost per month — the throughput the Tethers Unlimited design was sized for — and charges a service fee meaningfully below what the satellite operator currently pays for apogee propellant and the launch capacity to carry it, then at fifteen to twenty-five GEO launches per year globally, capturing a third of that market at a competitive service fee generates annual revenue in the range that justifies the facility's capital cost within a reasonable number of years. The facility itself, once launched, has no propellant operating cost. Its recurring costs are operations, tracking, and the occasional maintenance mission. Those margins are structurally different from a launch business.
The deorbit revenue from constellation operators adds a second stream that is lower per transaction but perhaps higher in volume. If constellation refresh rates continue as planned — and Starlink alone has stated a roughly five-year refresh cycle, implying hundreds of deorbits per year at current constellation size, rising steeply — then the deorbit business alone may fund some facility operations while the GEO boost business funds capital recovery. Deorbit customers supply momentum the facility can sell to boost customers; boost customers draw down the angular momentum that deorbit customers replenish. The facility is, in that sense, a clearinghouse — buying velocity cheap from dying satellites and selling it dear to ones that are just beginning their working lives.
The machine is not perpetual motion. It requires solar power for electrodynamic reboost, and it requires maintenance and operational oversight. But it does not require propellant. A Falcon 9 burns through roughly 400 tonnes of kerosene and liquid oxygen to reach orbit. This facility, once in place, burns through none — ever.

### The effort required

Building a MXER-class tether boost facility is a large engineering undertaking, but its scope is arguably more tractable than the development effort Rocket Lab has already committed to with Neutron.  Neutron has consumed an estimated $360 million or more through the end of 2025 and will require additional hundreds of millions to reach commercial operations, all to produce a partially reusable rocket whose core product—chemical propulsion—is well understood and intensely competed by SpaceX, ULA, and others.  A MXER tether boost facility, by contrast, would be harder physics but narrower engineering: a long rotating tether in a known orbit, a set of capture-and-release mechanisms, and an electrodynamic reboost system to replenish the momentum extracted from each payload.  The delta-v available per transfer could cover a meaningful fraction of the roughly 4.2 km/s needed to raise a satellite from LEO to GEO, making the facility genuinely useful to any operator who wants to reduce upper-stage mass or extend payload capacity.  And unlike Neutron, whose launch business will always be contested by better-capitalized rivals, a working tether boost facility would constitute a near-insurmountable moat: the orbit cannot be shared, the infrastructure cannot be quickly replicated, and the customers — every satellite operator who uses the service — become a recurring revenue stream on infrastructure Rocket Lab already owns.

A single MXER tether in LEO can carry most of the journey but not quite all of it. It throws its payload into a high elliptical transfer orbit whose apogee grazes GEO altitude, and then the satellite must fire its own apogee kick motor to circularize — a burn of roughly 1.5 km/s that still requires propellant, still costs launch mass, and still represents money left on the table.  The natural next step, once the first facility has proven itself and traffic has grown, would be a second rotating tether stationed in a high elliptical orbit, timed to catch payloads arriving from below and release them again at GEO velocity, effectively eliminating that final burn.  One analytical study found that such a two-stage architecture is actually lighter in total tether mass than a single‑stage system designed to do the whole job alone, because neither tether is asked to handle the full delta-v, and the stress on each is correspondingly lower.  The two-stage system is therefore not simply a luxury for a mature market; it is the logical completion of what the first tether begins, the second arch of a bridge that the first arch already implied.

## The ask

I want to end, as I have in the previous two essays, by being clear about what I am and what I am not, arguing.
I am not arguing that Rocket Lab should commit to building a tether boost facility. I am arguing that the company should commission a feasibility study — not a construction program, a study — that asks three questions. First: at what traffic level does the combined deorbit and GEO boost revenue justify the capital cost of a Neutron-launchable facility? Second: what is the minimum viable facility that could begin generating deorbit revenue while building the rendezvous and tether dynamics heritage needed for the boost service? Third: what would a partnership with Tethers Unlimited — a company Rocket Lab has already flown hardware for — look like as a structured development program rather than a one-off rideshare relationship?
Peter Beck has said publicly that he does not pursue technologies that do not have a credible commercial path. I think this one does, just. I think the commercial path runs through starting with the deorbit market, through the GEO boost market, and eventually — toward Venus and beyond. I think the company that first treats orbital velocity as inventory will have built something that is very hard to replicate once the traffic exists to support it.

The first customer pays Rocket Lab to remove momentum from a dying satellite. The second pays to buy that momentum back.

I own a small position in Rocket Lab. Not enough to matter to them, and probably not enough to matter to me. But I find myself, at seventy-something, doing orbital mechanics problems on a Thursday morning, trying to figure out whether a spinning cable in space could become a toll road. I don't know whether I'm right. I know the physics works. The rest is someone else's decision to make.

*The author is a retired architect, essayist, and Rocket Lab shareholder. He is currently studying spacecraft rendezvous and proximity operations at Johns Hopkins University. This is the third in a series of essays on the hidden physics of the space economy.*

Background: What is MXER?
The concept behind MXER—Momentum-Exchange/Electrodynamic Reboost—dates to the late 1990s and received serious NASA development attention in the early 2000s, when it was studied under the agency's In-Space Propulsion Technology program at Marshall Space Flight Center.  The core idea is elegant: a long rotating tether in an elliptical Earth orbit catches a payload traveling through low Earth orbit at the tip of its swing, then releases it at high velocity toward a higher orbit, transferring momentum much as a hammer thrower transfers energy to the ball.  The orbital energy the tether loses in each throw is then quietly replenished by driving electrical current through the cable using solar power; the current interacts with Earth's magnetic field to generate thrust, using the planet itself as the reaction mass and consuming no propellant at all.  Researchers from Tethers Unlimited and others described the facility as a "Panama Canal of space transportation"—permanent infrastructure capable of serving essentially every mission beyond LEO, scalable upward as traffic grew, and paying for itself after a small number of uses.  The Wikipedia and NASA articles on momentum exchange tethers gives a useful technical overview for readers who want to go deeper.
