# Between Orbits: MXER Tethers vs Electric Propulsion Tugs

## The Core Distinction

Two technologies are competing to fill the space between low Earth orbit and the higher-value destinations beyond it: solar-electric propulsion tugs and momentum-exchange/electrodynamic reboost (MXER) tether facilities. They are not simply alternative approaches to the same mission. They represent different philosophies about how transportation infrastructure should work in space — one is a reusable vehicle that burns propellant slowly, the other is a permanent installation that burns none at all.

An electric tug uses Hall thrusters or ion engines to spiral a client spacecraft from LEO to GEO or beyond over weeks or months. The specific impulse runs to 1,500–3,000 seconds — far more efficient than any chemical system — but thrust levels are low, millinewtons to a few newtons, so the transfer is slow. Efficiency and patience are the same transaction.

A MXER tether facility, by contrast, delivers its impulse impulsively — in a single grab-and-throw lasting seconds — and then uses electrodynamic interaction with Earth's magnetic field to restore the lost orbital energy using only solar power and ambient ionospheric plasma. The result is a system whose marginal cost per payload declines as it serves more customers, and whose propellant budget is effectively zero.

## The Time-Value Problem

For commercial satellite operators, time in transit is not a neutral cost. A GEO communications satellite earning revenue from the day it reaches station may generate tens of millions of dollars per month. A transfer that takes four months rather than four days represents real lost revenue, not merely inconvenience. Electric tugs accept that cost as the price of propellant efficiency; a tether facility eliminates it by delivering the payload to its transfer orbit almost instantly.

The operators who feel that cost most acutely are the ones running large revenue-generating comsats — a satellite earning tens of millions of dollars a month at GEO station is not indifferent to four months of transit. Government and defence customers have their own reasons to care about schedule. Science probes and cargo missions are less sensitive; a few months either way rarely changes the outcome. The likely result is not head-to-head competition but a natural sorting: electric tugs handle the patient missions, tether facilities handle the ones where time is money.

## Multiple Customer Segments: The Tether Advantage

The strongest commercial argument for a MXER facility is simpler than the physics: the same infrastructure serves several entirely distinct paying markets. GEO operators, lunar logistics providers, government science agencies — each has different budget cycles, different traffic cadences, and different reasons to care about propellantless transfer. A tug fleet scales costs with customers. A tether facility does not.

The GEO commercial market is the largest near-term opportunity: a MXER facility can eliminate or sharply reduce the liquid apogee engine a comsat needs to carry, with studies suggesting propellant mass savings of 40–60% per mission — at $8,000 or more per kilogram to GEO, that is a number satellite operators notice. But the same facility, by adjusting catch and release timing within the tether's rotation, can inject cargo into translunar trajectories as readily as it serves GEO — propellant depots, habitat modules, surface equipment delivered to the Moon without an upper stage. And for science missions constrained by launch mass, a tether boost covering even part of the required delta-v can transform a marginal proposal into a fundable one. Three markets, one piece of infrastructure, no propellant.

## Why the Multi-Segment Model Changes the Economics

A single-purpose LEO-to-GEO tether facility carries all fixed infrastructure cost against one revenue stream and is therefore vulnerable to demand cycles in the GEO market, which has been contracting as large LEO constellations take over some communications roles. A multi-destination facility spreads those fixed costs across GEO operators, lunar logistics companies, government science agencies, and defence customers with different budget cycles, procurement processes, and traffic cadences.

The electrodynamic reboost system replenishes the tether's energy continuously, and a well-sequenced mix of customers — GEO operators, lunar logistics runs, the occasional deep-space departure — keeps the facility closer to its design optimum than any single market could. A port that handles container ships, tankers, and ferries runs more efficiently than one waiting for container ships alone. The fixed costs don't change. The idle time does.

The result is a business structure more analogous to a port or a pipeline than to a fleet of vehicles. Once built, its marginal cost per additional payload is low, its competitive position is protected by the exclusive nature of its orbital slot and infrastructure, and its revenues diversify naturally as commercial and government space activity expands.

---

## Where Each System Wins

A tether facility is not the right tool for every mission. Its geometry constrains which orbits it can serve efficiently, and missions requiring fine-tuned phasing, exact slot delivery, or non-standard inclinations may still prefer an electric tug's flexibility. The most realistic near-term architecture is layered: a MXER facility handles the large, repeatable energy step from LEO to a standard transfer orbit, while electric tugs handle local routing, precise positioning, and the transfers that fall outside the tether's natural geometry. Each does what the other cannot.

The electric tug is a vehicle. The tether facility is a place. History suggests that places, once established, tend to persist — and that the economics of places, unlike the economics of vehicles, improve with time rather than wearing out. I don't know whether Rocket Lab will build this place. But I find it harder each week to explain why someone shouldn't.
