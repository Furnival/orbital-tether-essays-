**1. Momentum Is Not Free**
[Essay 1 of 4 on harvesting orbital momentum]
Why Rocket Lab's next business might not be launching rockets

Every time a constellation satellite re-enters the atmosphere, it converts thousands of dollars of carefully accumulated orbital velocity into a brief streak of light. We call that "deorbiting." Engineers call it controlled. I call it burning money — and momentum we could have kept.

I am not an aerospace engineer. I am a retired architect, Yale graduate, a former instructor at the Graduate School of Engineering at Quinnipiac University and Parsons Paris, and a shareholder in Rocket Lab since its SPAC days. I am currently taking a course in spacecraft rendezvous and proximity operations at Johns Hopkins, which means I am just far enough into this subject to know how much I don't know — and just far enough to recognize a structural inefficiency when I see one. What I see here is a system that has not yet decided whether orbital momentum is a resource or a waste product. I think that decision is coming. And I think it matters which company makes it first.


## Treating velocity as if it were free
One of the odd things about reading orbital mechanics as an outsider is how casual the language can sound around velocity. We "raise" and "lower" orbits as if we were adjusting a desk chair. We "de-orbit" of satellites, which is an oddly gentle verb for sending a few hundred kilograms of carefully engineered hardware into a plasma-bright fire. The mathematics underneath is precise, but the vocabulary can make it sound as if orbital momentum can be casually discarded.
It shouldn't be. A satellite in low Earth orbit sits on top of thousands of meters per second of carefully purchased velocity. That delta-v represents launch vehicle complexity, propellant, guidance, manufacturing, insurance — in short, money. At the end of the satellite's life, we currently take that paid-for momentum and turn it into a streak of light and some additional heating in the upper atmosphere. The industry calls that "controlled reentry" and counts it as responsible behavior. The architect in me has trouble not seeing it as waste baked into the design.
There is a second problem hiding inside the first. When a satellite burns up, it does not simply disappear. A typical 250-kilogram satellite generates roughly thirty kilograms of aluminum oxide nanoparticles that linger in the stratosphere for years. We are currently planning to deorbit tens of thousands of constellation satellites over the next few decades. Nobody is entirely sure what that accumulation does to the atmosphere that protects us. The comfortable assumption — that burning things up is the clean solution — is beginning to look less comfortable.


## A business hiding in the waste stream
When we decided that throwing away a first stage on every flight was no longer acceptable, we didn't do it because the physics had changed. We did it because someone finally treated the rocket stage as an asset instead of as disposable packaging. The same story is now playing out with servicing missions in GEO: a dead satellite is no longer a piece of orbital debris; it is something you can dock with and revive for a fee. In both cases, the commercial moment arrived when the industry stopped assuming that loss was inevitable.
Momentum-exchange tethers feel to me like an invitation to push that logic one level deeper. In the tether literature, a MXER facility is a rotating system that can transfer velocity from one spacecraft to another. That sounds exotic until you translate it into the language of services: a shared piece of orbital infrastructure that buys low-value momentum from satellites that are finished with it and sells high-value momentum to satellites that still need it. On the input side, you have a deorbit obligation that would otherwise be pure cost. On the output side, you have a boost that would otherwise require propellant and the mass and complexity that come with it.
The physics is worth pausing on, even briefly. A tether joining two satellites in adjacent orbits into a rotovator does not simply hang between them like a rope. It rotates — the whole system spins, slowly, around its common center of mass. When the lower end captures a satellite, the satellite’s momentum is absorbed into the spin of the system, storing the momentum in rotation. When the upper end releases a payload, it flings it forward, drawing on that stored rotation. Nothing is created or destroyed; the momentum transfers. The tether is not a rope. It is a flywheel.
And we can think in business terms thus: this momentum is an unpriced asset, in a great location.
I find the regenerative braking analogy useful here, not because it is technically precise, but because it captures the structural logic. Electric cars do not generate energy; they recover energy that would otherwise be lost as heat in the brake pads. A momentum-exchange tether does not create delta-v; it recovers delta-v that would otherwise be lost as heat in the upper atmosphere. In both cases, the engineering insight is the same: if you are going to slow down anyway, you might as well keep something.
I do not know whether that trade can be made profitable at current launch prices and constellation sizes. I am still learning the equations that would let me even guess. But I have been an architect long enough to recognize a pattern: when a system routinely throws away something it had to work hard to create -- eventually someone finds a way to stand in that waste stream and charge for catching it.
Why this looks like a Rocket Lab question to me
This is where my position as a shareholder colors how I read the problem. There are only a handful of companies that both launch rockets and operate their own spacecraft. There are fewer still that have already flown tether-based deorbit hardware for customers and are building a larger vehicle capable of placing more ambitious infrastructure in orbit. From where I sit, Rocket Lab looks uncomfortably like the company that is going to have this opportunity whether it wants it or not.
I am not suggesting they pivot tomorrow into "momentum banking." I am suggesting there is a plausible sequence of businesses that all point in that direction. At the near end: small tether and drag-enhancement missions that sell cleaner, more predictable deorbit services to constellation operators who are beginning to worry about what thousands of burn-ups might be doing to the upper atmosphere. One layer up: a rotating facility — something like a rotovator — that earns revenue by giving expired satellites a gentler path down, possibly gentle enough to make inflatable heat shields and partial hardware recovery practical. Only beyond that does a full MXER-style system emerge, one that can also sell boosts to new satellites as traffic grows.
Then there is a direction the sequence doesn't usually mention: outward. Rocket Lab has already declared a serious interest in Venus — they have spoken publicly for a private Venus atmospheric mission using Photon. What is rarely discussed is that the deep-gravity-well problem Venus presents on the return leg is, in structural terms, the same problem that motivates MXER in the first place: you arrive with momentum you cannot easily shed, and you depart needing momentum you cannot easily acquire. A tether system capable of catching an incoming spacecraft and transferring its velocity to an outbound one — rather than burning both away as propellant and heat — is exactly the kind of infrastructure that makes repeated Venus operations less punishing. A company that has learned momentum exchange in LEO with constellation satellites will have built the guidance systems, the catch-and-release dynamics, and the tether material heritage that a Venus-class deep-space application eventually demands. The markets are different in scale; the engineering problems rhyme.
If that sequence is even roughly right, there is a quiet strategic question on the table: will Rocket Lab use some fraction of its existing flights to start learning how to do this? SpaceX did not announce "fully reusable rockets" at the outset; it started by awkwardly trying to land first stages on barges and learning from the splashes. I do not have the authority — or the equations — to tell Rocket Lab what to do. I can only say that, as a student of orbital mechanics and as someone who has watched other industries learn to stop throwing away value, I would feel better knowing that somewhere on a manifest there is a small, experimental tether whose main purpose is not revenue, but curiosity.


## A shareholder's question, held lightly
I have quite a few shares of Rocket Lab. That does not give me any authority, but it does give me a reason to pay attention. What I have tried to do in this essay is not to write a business plan or a technical proposal, but to ask a question in public that I suspect a few people inside that company are already asking in private: at what point does momentum stop being something we discard and start being something we sell? I don't know the answer. I'm not sure anyone does yet. But I think the company that first treats orbital velocity as inventory, rather than exhaust, will have found one of the more durable businesses in the space economy — and I would prefer it to be one I own a small piece of.


*The author is a retired architect, essayist, and Rocket Lab shareholder. He is currently studying spacecraft rendezvous and proximity operations at Johns Hopkins University.*
